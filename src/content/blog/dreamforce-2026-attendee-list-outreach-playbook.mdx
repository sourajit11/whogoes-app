## Who Shows Up at Dreamforce (and Why It Matters for Your Sequences)

Not everyone at Dreamforce is the same buyer. Not even close. The 40,000+ attendees break into distinct segments, and your outreach needs to match.

**Salesforce Admins and Architects** make up a huge chunk of the audience. They're there for Trailhead workshops, hands-on labs, and certification exams. They don't sign contracts, but they influence every technology purchase that touches Salesforce. If you sell anything that integrates with the platform, these are your internal champions.

**ISV Partners and AppExchange Vendors** attend to network, recruit, and showcase. They know the ecosystem cold. Generic pitches bounce off them. You need to reference their specific product category or the partner track sessions they're attending.

**Enterprise AEs and Sales Leaders** come for the keynotes, the executive networking events, and the Ohana culture. They're evaluating tools for their own teams. If you sell to sales orgs, this is your segment.

**C-Suite and VP-Level Executives** are fewer in number but disproportionately valuable. Industry attendee data consistently puts roughly 60% of Dreamforce attendees at Director-level or above, which is unusually senior for an event that also packs in tens of thousands of hands-on practitioners walking the same halls. That mix is exactly why segmentation matters here.

**RevOps and Marketing Ops** professionals round out the mix. They're comparing workflows, evaluating automation, and sitting in the data and analytics tracks.

I've seen [SDR teams](/blog/trade-show-attendee-data-sdrs) blast the same email to all five segments. The results are predictably bad. Segment first, write second.

## Your Four-Week Dreamforce Outreach Calendar

Timing isn't a nice-to-have. It's the whole game. The idea is simple: warm people up while their inboxes are still quiet, build relevance as the agenda firms up, and only ask for the meeting once they are actively mapping out who to see at Moscone. Here's the cadence I'd run.

### Week 1: Four Weeks Out (August 18)

This is your awareness window. Nobody's thinking about meetings yet. They're booking flights and hotels.

**Goal:** Get on their radar. No hard ask.

**Email angle:** Share something useful. A session recommendation list, a San Francisco dinner guide for Dreamforce week, a quick take on the Agentic Enterprise theme. The point is to be helpful before you're salesy.

**Template:**

```
Subject: Spotted you're headed to Dreamforce

Hey [First Name],

Saw your post about attending Dreamforce this year. [Company]'s
session on [topic] looks solid.

We're helping [similar companies] with [your value prop] and I
put together a quick list of the Agentforce sessions most
relevant to [their role/industry]. Want me to send it over?

Either way, have a great trip.

[Your name]
```

Short. No pitch. Just a human being useful. That's it.

### Week 2: Three Weeks Out (August 25)

**Goal:** Establish relevance. Connect your product to something they care about at the event.

**Email angle:** Reference a specific session track or keynote topic and tie it to a problem your product solves. The Agentforce 360 sessions and the "Becoming an Agentic Enterprise" theme give you plenty to work with.

### Week 3: Two Weeks Out (September 1)

**Goal:** Ask for the meeting. This is your conversion email.

**Email angle:** Direct. Specific time and place. "Coffee at Moscone on Tuesday at 2pm?" beats "Let's find time to connect." People are locking in their schedules now.

**Template:**

```
Subject: Quick coffee at Dreamforce? (re: Agentforce)

[First Name],

I know your calendar's filling up, so I'll keep this short.

We've been working with [2-3 similar companies] on [specific
use case tied to Dreamforce theme], and I think there's a fit
with what [Company] is doing.

Could we grab 15 minutes at Moscone? I'm free Tuesday 2-4pm
or Wednesday morning.

If not, no pressure. Enjoy the keynote.

[Your name]
```

### Week 4: Week Of (September 15-17)

**Goal:** Last-chance meeting requests + real-time engagement.

**Email angle:** Keep it to one sentence. Everyone's inbox is overflowing. "Still have 15 min tomorrow? Moscone West lobby, 10am." If they don't respond, that's fine. You've already built familiarity over three weeks.

### Post-Event: Days 1-5 After (September 18-22)

Don't skip this. According to [CEIR](https://www.ceir.org), leads contacted within 48 hours of an event convert at much higher rates than those left for a week or more. Reference something specific from the conference: the Benioff keynote, an Agentforce demo, a party they posted about.

<Callout type="tip">
The biggest mistake I see teams make: waiting until the week of the event to start outreach. By then, attendees have 200+ emails from vendors. Your four-week runway is what separates you from the noise.
</Callout>

## Subject Lines That Reference Dreamforce's 2026 Themes

Generic subject lines die at Dreamforce. "Great connecting at Dreamforce" is what every vendor sends. Your subject line needs to prove you actually know what's happening at the event, and the fastest way to signal that is to name a specific keynote, session, or product announcement that a given segment genuinely cares about rather than the conference in the abstract.

The 2026 theme is "Becoming an Agentic Enterprise," centered on [Agentforce 360](https://www.salesforce.com/dreamforce/) and autonomous AI agents. Use that.

**Pre-show subject lines (tie to sessions and themes):**

- "Your take on Agentforce 360? (before Dreamforce)"
- "The Agentic Enterprise session + [your product angle]"
- "[First Name], quick question about your Salesforce stack"
- "Dreamforce prep: [specific session name] looks relevant for [Company]"
- "Spotted [Company] is exhibiting at Dreamforce"

**Day-of subject lines (short, urgent):**

- "At Moscone? 15 min tomorrow?"
- "Just left the Agentforce keynote, thought of [Company]"
- "Dreamforce is chaos, but quick coffee?"

**Post-show subject lines (reference shared experience):**

- "Post-Dreamforce: the Agentforce demo was wild"
- "Back from SF, one thought on [Company]'s setup"
- "Benioff's keynote + what it means for [their use case]"

<Callout type="tip">
Subject lines under 7 words outperform longer ones for event outreach. "At Moscone? Quick chat?" beats "I'd love to schedule a brief meeting at Dreamforce to discuss synergies." Every time.
</Callout>

## Personalizing by Attendee Segment: What to Say to Whom

This is where most playbooks fall apart. They give you one template. One size fits none. The same words that make an admin lean in (API names, integration specifics, the right Trailhead module) will read as noise to a CRO who only wants to know whether you can move pipeline this quarter. Different angles, every time.

| Segment | Pain Point to Reference | Session Track to Mention | Email Tone |
|---|---|---|---|
| Salesforce Admins | Integration complexity, manual workflows | Hands-on labs, Trailhead sessions | Technical, peer-to-peer |
| ISV Partners | AppExchange visibility, co-selling | Partner track, ecosystem keynotes | Business development, partnership |
| Enterprise AEs | Pipeline velocity, quota attainment | Sales Cloud sessions, AI for Sales | Direct, ROI-focused |
| C-Suite / VPs | Digital transformation, AI strategy | Main stage keynotes, executive networking | Strategic, concise |
| RevOps / Marketing Ops | Data quality, attribution, automation | Data Cloud sessions, analytics track | Process-oriented, metrics-driven |

A few specifics worth knowing. Salesforce admins respond to technical detail. Name the API, name the integration, name the Trailhead module. They'll ignore anything that sounds like marketing copy.

ISV partners, on the other hand, want to hear about co-selling and ecosystem reach. Mention AppExchange rankings or partner program tiers and you'll get their attention.

For the C-suite attendees, keep it to three sentences max. They're not reading a four-paragraph email between keynotes. "Saw [Company] is at Dreamforce. We help [similar company] do [specific thing]. Worth 10 minutes?" That's enough.

## Reading LinkedIn Proof Before You Write Your First Email

This is something I don't see enough teams do. Before you send a single email, read the LinkedIn post that confirms the person is attending. It's right there in your [attendee list](/blog/what-is-a-trade-show-attendee-list).

Why? Because the post itself is an outreach goldmine. If someone wrote "Excited to check out the Agentforce demos at Dreamforce this year," you now have a conversation starter that isn't generic. "Saw your post about Agentforce" hits different than "I see you're attending Dreamforce."

Some attendees post about specific sessions they're excited for. Others mention they're speaking, exhibiting, or bringing their team. Each of those details is a personalization hook. It's free intel. Reading the post first costs you about thirty seconds and hands you an opening line that no purchased list, however expensive, could ever match, because it comes straight from the attendee's own words. Your mileage may vary, but I've seen this one detail bump reply rates by double digits.

Scan the LinkedIn post before writing your email. One specific detail from their post ("saw you're excited about the Data Cloud keynote") is worth more than any amount of generic conference small talk.

For SDRs, the exhibitor list doubles as a prospecting list. The people staffing those booths are product managers, sales leaders, and partnership directors. They're accessible, they're in buying mode for their own operations, and they've publicly committed to being there.
