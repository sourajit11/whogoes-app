## What Aerospace and Defence Buyers Want at Farnborough 2026

Farnborough isn't a general tech conference where attendees browse for inspiration. People come with specific procurement mandates. The six [official themes](https://www.farnboroughairshow.com/the-show/themes/) map directly to what buyers are evaluating. No browsing. All business.

**Global Security buyers** are looking at next-gen defence platforms, multi-domain C4ISR systems, and counter-drone technology. With geopolitical tensions driving defence budgets upward across NATO nations, the procurement officers walking the defence halls aren't window-shopping. They're comparing capabilities against active RFPs.

**Advanced Technology and AI buyers** care about predictive maintenance, digital twins for fleet management, autonomous systems, and AI-driven mission planning. These are the avionics engineers and CTO-types evaluating vendor capabilities against specific integration requirements.

**Supply Chain buyers** are the ones feeling the heat from post-pandemic production ramp-ups. Boeing and Airbus both have massive order backlogs, and their Tier 1 and Tier 2 suppliers are scrambling to scale. Big backlogs. Tight timelines. Procurement leads from these suppliers are at Farnborough evaluating new sources for castings, composites, and machined parts.

**Sustainability buyers** are tracking SAF (sustainable aviation fuel) providers, hydrogen propulsion startups, and electric taxi systems. Growing fast. Airlines under pressure from EU emissions regulations need solutions, and Farnborough is where they evaluate them.

The Finance and Future Workforce themes round out the picture, but from a sales prospecting standpoint, the first four are where the money moves. If you map your target accounts to those four themes before the show, you can prioritize which halls to walk, which sessions to monitor for buying signals, and which conversations are worth chasing across the trade-only days when serious buyers actually have time to talk.

<Callout type="tip">
If you're selling into aerospace supply chains, focus your outreach on the Supply Chain and Advanced Technology tracks. Buyers in these sessions are actively solving production bottleneck problems and have near-term purchase timelines.
</Callout>

## The Farnborough Attendee Profile That Most SDRs Overlook

The obvious targets at Farnborough are airline CEOs and defence ministers. Every sales team targets them. Good luck getting a meeting.

The profiles worth your time are one or two levels below the headline names. These are the people who actually evaluate, recommend, and sign purchase orders. Once you shift your targeting down a rung from the ministers and chief executives that every other sales team is chasing, you start finding the procurement managers, technical evaluators, and operations directors who control real budgets and actually reply to a well-researched message.

**MRO Directors and VP Operations.** Airlines and lessors send their maintenance leadership to evaluate engine MRO providers, component repair shops, and PBH (power-by-the-hour) contracts. These buyers have authority over multi-year service agreements worth tens of millions. They don't get mobbed at cocktail receptions the way C-suite executives do.

**Defence Programme Managers.** Not the generals or the attaches (though they're there too). The programme managers running specific acquisition programmes are the ones comparing solutions against requirements documents. In the UK, that's often someone from DE&S (Defence Equipment and Support). For US delegations, think PEOs (Program Executive Officers) and their deputies.

**Avionics and Systems Engineers.** These are the technical evaluators who'll tell their programme manager whether your system integrates with existing platforms. They attend specific sessions, often in the Advanced Technology and AI track, and spend time at booths asking detailed technical questions. If your product is technical, these are your gatekeepers. Full stop.

**Airline Fleet Planning Analysts.** They're not the CEO announcing a billion-dollar order on stage. They're the analysts who spent the previous six months modelling fleet scenarios, and they're at Farnborough validating assumptions with OEMs and lessors. Worth knowing.

**Space Sector Startup Founders.** Farnborough's Space Zone has grown in each edition. Founders of small satellite companies, launch providers, and space data analytics firms attend looking for partners, investors, and customers. If you're selling infrastructure or services to the space sector, this is a concentrated audience.

<Callout type="tip">
Government delegations at Farnborough typically include 3-5 decision-makers per country, not just the figurehead. The junior officials accompanying a defence minister are often the ones running active procurement programmes, and they're far more accessible.
</Callout>

## Reading Buyer Signals at Farnborough: What Separates Hot Leads from Browsers

Not everyone at FIA 2026 is buying. Plenty of people come for the flying displays or to network without any procurement authority. Sorting real buyers from the crowd is the hard part. Here's what to watch for. The trick is reading the small signals that separate a procurement officer with an active requirement from a curious engineer killing time between flying displays, because those signals sit scattered across LinkedIn posts, session rosters, and chalet bookings if you know where to look.

**RFP timing.** Defence procurement follows budget cycles. UK MOD programmes typically release RFPs in Q1 or Q3. If someone from a UK defence programme is at Farnborough in late July, they're either finalizing requirements for a Q3 RFP or doing early market research for the following year. Either way, worth a conversation. The US DoD follows a similar rhythm tied to the fiscal year (October start).

**Fleet renewal cycles.** Airlines don't buy aircraft on impulse. Fleet planning happens 3-7 years out. But the components, systems, and service contracts that support new fleet types get evaluated closer to delivery. If an airline has A321XLRs or 737 MAX 10s arriving in 2027-2028, their MRO and systems procurement teams are at Farnborough now. That's a signal.

**LinkedIn posting behaviour.** Someone posting "Excited to attend FIA 2026" is interesting. Someone posting "Looking forward to evaluating sustainable propulsion solutions at Farnborough" is better. Big difference. The specificity of the post tells you something about intent. This is exactly what WhoGoes captures: public LinkedIn posts mentioning the event, giving you [verified proof of attendance](/blog/how-to-verify-trade-show-attendance) plus a window into what they care about.

**Session attendance patterns.** The [FIA summits](https://www.farnboroughairshow.com/) (Defence Summit, Airline Leaders Summit, Finance Summit, Technology Summit) are invite-only or paid-admission. People who attend those aren't casual visitors. Not even close. If you can identify summit attendees, you've filtered for seniority and intent simultaneously.

**Chalet bookings.** This is old aerospace sales knowledge, but it holds. Companies that book chalets at Farnborough are spending upward of six figures for the week. They're there to do business. The chalet row is where the real deals happen, not the exhibition floor. If a company has a chalet, their senior leadership is on-site and meeting-ready.

## Farnborough vs. Paris Air Show: Different Crowds, Different Opportunities

These two shows alternate years (Farnborough in even years, Paris Le Bourget in odd), and most aerospace professionals attend both. But the attendee mix isn't identical. Not by a long shot.

| | Farnborough 2024 | Paris Air Show 2025 |
|---|---|---|
| Trade visitors | 74,000+ ([FIA](https://www.farnboroughairshow.com/)) | 210,000+ ([SIAE](https://www.siae.fr/)) |
| Total visitors (incl. public) | 100,358 | 320,000+ |
| Exhibitors | 1,500 | 2,453 |
| Countries | 60+ | 48+ |
| Orders value | $105.8B | $150B+ |
| Defence emphasis | Strong (UK MOD, NATO allies) | Strong (French MOD, EU defence) |
| Space presence | Growing Space Zone | CNES-anchored, more mature |

**Paris is bigger.** No question. More exhibitors, more visitors, more total order value. But Farnborough's trade-only days (Monday through Wednesday) create a more concentrated B2B environment. The ratio of serious buyers to general visitors is higher during those first three days.

**Defence skew differs.** Farnborough leans toward UK MOD, Five Eyes nations, and Gulf state delegations. Paris leans toward EU defence cooperation, French MOD programmes, and African/Middle Eastern government buyers. If your customer base is predominantly UK, US, or Gulf, Farnborough is your show. Paris covers more of continental Europe and francophone Africa.

**Deal-making culture.** I've heard from aerospace sales leads that Farnborough chalets are where handshake agreements happen, while Paris is more about the public announcement. Not a hard rule, but the intimate scale of Farnborough's chalet row makes it easier to get face time with senior buyers. Smaller crowd, more access.

**Space sector.** Paris has a more established space presence anchored by CNES and ESA. Farnborough's Space Zone is growing, but it's still catching up. If you're focused on space, Paris offers more density. For everything else in commercial aviation and defence, Farnborough delivers.

Side note: because the shows alternate, many aerospace companies plan their outreach on a two-year cycle. If you missed Paris 2025, Farnborough 2026 is your next window. Don't wait for Paris 2027. Treating the two airshows as a single rolling campaign instead of two isolated events lets you nurture the same aerospace and defence buyers across eighteen months and arrive at each show already familiar to the procurement teams you most want to meet.
