> **Quick answer:** An event attendee list is a verified database of professionals who attended or plan to attend a specific trade show, including names, titles, companies, and contact details. Unlike generic contact databases, attendee lists carry proof of attendance (such as public LinkedIn posts) making outreach timely and relevant.

## What Is an Event Attendee List?

An **event attendee list** is a curated collection of verified contacts who attended or plan to attend a specific trade show or conference.

The important word there is *specific*. This isn't a generic industry database. It's tied to CES 2026, [HIMSS 2026](/events/himss-2026), NRF 2026, or whatever event you're targeting. And ideally, it includes proof that each person was actually there.

## Key Takeaways

- Attendee lists give you names, companies, titles, and verified emails for people who actually attended a specific event
- LinkedIn proof of attendance (public posts mentioning the event) is more reliable than organizer registration data
- Attendee lists are far more cost-effective than organizer lists or enterprise intent data platforms
- Speed matters: following up within 48 hours of an event drives the best response rates

## Why Attendee Lists Matter for Sales Teams

If you sell B2B software and your prospects attend trade shows, you already know the pain:

- **Before the event:** You want to book meetings with the right people, but you don't know who's going.
- **During the event:** You meet 20 people at a booth, collect business cards, and lose half of them.
- **After the event:** You want to follow up with everyone who attended, but the organizer's list costs $5,000-$20,000 (if they sell it at all).

An attendee list solves all three. You get names, companies, titles, and verified emails, ready for outreach.

I've talked to SDR teams who spend their entire first day at a trade show wandering the floor hoping to bump into the right people. That's expensive improvisation. With an attendee list, you walk in with a calendar full of meetings already booked.

## The Problem with Manual LinkedIn Searching

The most common alternative is [manually searching LinkedIn](/compare/whogoes-vs-manual-linkedin-search). You type "CES 2026" in the search bar, scroll through posts, and copy-paste contact details into a spreadsheet.

It works. But it takes **4+ hours per event**, and you're at the mercy of LinkedIn's algorithm. You only see a fraction of the people who actually posted. I've seen teams build lists this way for a single event and feel productive, only to realize they missed hundreds of attendees who simply didn't show up in their search results.

For a single event, it's tedious. For 10+ events per quarter, it's completely unsustainable.

## What "LinkedIn Proof" Actually Means

Not all attendee lists are equal. The differentiator that matters most is **proof of attendance**.

Traditional attendee lists come from event organizers. The problem? They include:
- People who registered but never showed up
- People who attended virtually but weren't really engaged
- Outdated contact info from registration forms filled out months ago

According to [CEIR (Center for Exhibition Industry Research)](https://www.ceir.org), roughly 20-30% of registrants at major trade shows are no-shows. That's a big chunk of dead contacts you'd be paying for.

**LinkedIn proof** is different. Each contact has a public LinkedIn post about the event: a photo from the booth, a comment about a keynote, a status update saying "excited for next week." That's real-time, first-party evidence they were there and engaged.

When you reach out and say "I saw you were at CES. What did you think of the AI keynote?", it's genuine. The response rate goes up because the context is real, not fabricated.

## How Attendee Lists Compare to Event Organizer Lists

Event organizers sometimes sell their attendee lists directly. The comparison:

- **Cost:** Organizer lists run $5,000-$20,000 per event. WhoGoes starts at $29 for 200 contacts.
- **Availability:** Not all organizers sell lists. Many won't share data at all, especially post-GDPR.
- **Proof:** Organizer lists include registrants, not just attendees. The [CEIR](https://www.ceir.org) data on 20-30% no-show rates means you're paying a premium for contacts who weren't even in the building.
- **Speed:** Organizer lists arrive weeks after the event. LinkedIn-sourced lists are available during it.

The speed gap is the killer. By the time an organizer sends you their list, your competitors have already followed up.

## How to Use an Attendee List for Outreach

Once you have your attendee list, the framework is straightforward:

1. **Pre-event (2-4 weeks before):** Filter the list by title and company size. Send personalized emails: "I see you're heading to NRF. Want to grab coffee at our booth?"

2. **During the event:** Cross-reference the list with people you met. Add notes about conversations. Prioritize follow-ups.

3. **Post-event (within 48 hours):** Send follow-ups referencing the event: "Great seeing you at NRF. You mentioned you're looking at [topic]. Here's how we can help."

The 48-hour part isn't arbitrary. According to the [Trade Show News Network (TSNN)](https://www.tsnn.com), trade show leads that receive follow-up within that window convert at much higher rates than those contacted a week later.
